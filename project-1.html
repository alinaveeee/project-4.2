Introduction

UX is a traditionally Western field. As it becomes more popular and practiced internationally, many researchers have questioned whether UX research methods are as effective in non-Western cultures. Existing research suggests that culture does have an impact on UX research methods. However, many of these studies compare methods across broad, international groups. As a result, their findings are limited to the hegemonic cultural groups of each country. Additionally, while there are studies that compare UX research methods across cultural groups within an individual country, these studies do not occur in the West. In general, there is an assumption that traditional UX research methods are effective across all of the West when there is a lack of research supporting this.

To begin to address this gap, I tested three UX research methods with Black, Asian, and Latinx women. First, participants were asked to think aloud, complete a survey, and participate in an interview as part of a typical usability study. Then, in a follow-up interview, we discussed their experiences with each of the three methods. These discussions, combined with my observations, suggest that Black, Asian, and Latinx women experience UX research methods differently.

Literature Review

What do we know about culture and UX research methods?

To give a quick recap of existing research, I've identified two types of studies that deal with culture and UX research methods. The first type of study is what I call the “international study.” International studies test and compare UX research methods across multiple countries. For example, a 2002 study by Evers tested the think aloud, survey, and interview with English, North American, Dutch, and Japanese participants.

The second type of study is what I call the “national study.” These studies are similar in that they test UX research methods across cultural groups. However, national studies differ because they focus on cultural groups within one country. A 2011 study by Hsieh, for example, tested UX research methods with younger and older Taiwanese participants. There have been similar studies in Malaysia, India, and Namibia that compare age, gender, and class groups.

Both national and international studies have found that culture does have an impact on the efficacy of UX research methods. Evers found that surveys posed problems for English, Dutch, and Japanese participants. Hsieh found that younger Taiwanese participants were more likely to give feedback and criticism than older Taiwanese participants.

Discussion

With that being said, there are some issues to consider with the research so far.

First, international studies lack specificity. Because they are comparing such broad groups, it's important to question who exactly is being represented. Who were the “North American” participants in Evers' study? What does all of North America have in common that the entire continent was being compared to individual countries? As a Black and Mexican-American person, these were my first questions. Later, I realized that Evers was referring to US Americans. Although I was initially mistaken, there was still a question of representation. And, while I first asked this question of Evers' study, it is a valid question for any of the international studies. How well were marginalized groups represented in these studies?

Mock Usability Study

First, we conducted a mock usability study. We tested connectie.org using the think aloud, survey, and interview methods.

Follow-Up Interview

In a follow-up interview, I asked participants to rank each of the methods from favorite to least favorite. Ranking the methods opened up the discussion so that participants could explain what they liked and didn't like about each of the methods. It also helped avoid framing any of the methods as problematic.

After ranking the methods, we discussed the role of culture in the study. Although I had observations, it was important to validate them with my participants.

Findings

Are there cultural differences within the US?

Black, Asian, and Latinx women believe culture can have an impact

After trying all of the methods, everyone said they believed culture could have an impact. They described ways they could imagine a person's culture influencing their behavior and comfort during the study. For example, multiple participants mentioned politeness in East Asian culture and suggested that East Asians may not feel as comfortable giving criticism.

Responses to the question, "Can you see ways in which your culture played a role in the study?"

Latinx women

Although everyone believed culture could have an impact, only Latinx women believed that their culture actually played a role in the study. They expressed concerns over whether their thoughts were important enough to say aloud. For example, they were unsure if expressing their opinion about the color palette was relevant to the study. They attributed this to their upbringing not just as Latinx people, but specifically as Latinx women. Although this did not have a major impact on their participation, it was a concern in the back of their mind that might be best described as tension.

In contrast to the think aloud, the interview eased this tension since they were directly asked for their thoughts and opinions. Being prompted by questions and asked to elaborate reassured them that their thoughts were important— even when it came to seemingly minor things like color.

Black and Latinx women appeared to fear judgment

Latinx women

Throughout our discussions in the follow-up interview, Black and Latinx women expressed concerns that their tech-savviness was being tested. During the think aloud, they were worried that making a mistake on the website would reflect poorly on them. Although we were there to test the website, they felt a bit like they were the ones being tested.

Conclusion

What does all of this mean?

Overall, none of the methods tested in this study posed significant problems for Black, Asian, or Latinx women. In general, all of the methods were described as easy, with the interview being ranked the most favorite across all women. The think aloud was ranked second. Although it was unfamiliar, many women enjoyed the freedom of saying all of their thoughts uninterrupted. The survey ranked last, mainly out of indifference. It was described as straightforward, but limited and forgettable.

Still, Black and Latinx women did experience some tension during the think aloud. This suggests that Black and Latinx women may hesitate to fully participate in the UX research process.
